The 3 Questions

Question 1 — Did they actually work with you?
This is the foundational verification gate.

"Can you confirm that [First Name] worked with you or your organisation?"
○ Yes, I can confirm this
○ No, I can't confirm this

Simple. Binary. This alone gives the profile a "Work History Confirmed" badge. If they click No, the request is quietly flagged for review — no drama.

Question 2 — How did they perform?
This captures the reliability signal employers actually care about.

"How would you describe [First Name]'s reliability and professionalism?"
○ ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Excellent — I'd hire them again without hesitation
○ ⭐⭐⭐⭐ Good — solid performer, no major issues
○ ⭐⭐⭐ Mixed — some positives but also some concerns
○ Would prefer not to say


💡 Note: "Would prefer not to say" is critical. It gives referees an honest exit that isn't a damaging public negative — you handle anything below ⭐⭐⭐⭐ carefully behind the scenes.


Question 3 — The money question for employers
This is the one hiring managers actually want answered.

"Would you work with [First Name] again?"
○ Absolutely — already have / would without hesitation
○ Yes, given the right role
○ Unlikely
○ Prefer not to say

The Optional 4th Field
Not a question — a text box, completely optional:

"Anything else you'd like to add about working with [First Name]? (Optional — this may be shown on their profile)"

Keep it optional and low pressure. When referees do fill it in, these short quotes become the most powerful thing on a freelancer's profile — authentic, specific, from a named company.

Critical Design Details

The entire form should live on one page, no login required for the referee
Pre-fill everything possible — their name, the freelancer's name, the event if known
Mobile-first design — most referees will open this on their phone
Add a "This will take 45 seconds" line at the top — it dramatically increases completion rates
Send a confirmation email to the freelancer when a reference is completed so they feel the momentum
